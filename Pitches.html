Pitch 1: Natural gas pipeline incidents

In the last decade the U.S. has experienced a natural gas boom mainly because of the discovery of large shale gas reserves. But it also partly due to the technological advances in hydraulic fracturing — or fracking — that have allowed the exploitation of natural gas to become a commercially viable option.

These datasets from the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA) provide a list of incident reports from 1984 to 2013. I would like to see how incidents have changed over the years. I believe the data will show an increase in incidents simply because the mileage of pipelines has increased. But I also expect that the type of incidents would have changed as industry practices change. I think there is a wealth of information in these datasets. Here are some questions that I’d like to get answers for:

1. Which operator has had the highest number of incidents?

2. Which state has had the most incidents/per mile of pipeline?

3. Which PHMSA department has the most incidents/ mile of pipeline? PHMSA has offices that take care of the southwest, central, western and eastern regions. Each office is responsible for conducting safety reviews. If one office has a large share of incident reports it might indicate sloppiness on PHMSA’s part.

Pitch 2: Fracking and earthquakes

Seismologists and environmentalists have long been warning that fracking can lead to earthquakes. I’d like to test this theory out using data from IRIS and a state fracking report. Combining these two data sets to see if there has been a rise in earthquakes reported that matches the fracking boom might make a stronger case for this theory.
